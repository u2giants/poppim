# How the Business Works — POP Creations & Spruce Line

**Purpose.** This document explains how this company actually designs, approves, and finishes a product — who touches it, in what order, through every level of approval (including licensors), from a first idea to a finished, shipped item. It also records how the people who run the work *wish* the process ran. It is written for someone (human or AI) who needs to understand the **business and its workflow**. It deliberately contains **nothing about the software** used to track the work — no tools, no databases, no tickets. The companion document `pm-system-design.md` covers the software.

**Where this knowledge comes from.** 77 structured interview answers across four rounds (May–June 2026) with the three people who run the two lines:
- **Jessica Cortázar** — Project Manager, POP Creations (the licensed line).
- **Liz (Elizabeth) Parkin** — Creative Director, POP Creations.
- **Jennifer (Jen) Chaffier** — Creative Director, Spruce Line (the generic line).

Quotes are verbatim and attributed by person.

---

## 1. What the company does

The company **designs and sources home decor products** — wall art, framed and stretched canvas, plaques, storage (soft and hard), floor coverings (coir mats), garden items, tabletop objects, clocks, photo frames, desk accessories — which are **manufactured in China and imported to the United States** for sale to major retail chains.

**The buyers (retailers):** Burlington, the TJX family (HomeGoods, Marshalls, TJ Maxx), Ross, Hobby Lobby, Walmart, Dollar General, Amazon, Hot Topic, Box Lunch, Kohl's, Five Below, At Home, Costco, DD's, Ollies, and others. Each retailer assigns specific **buyers** (named individuals) who choose what their store will carry.

The company runs **two parallel businesses** that share people and factories but follow fundamentally different processes. Understanding this split is the single most important thing about the company.

| | **POP Creations** | **Spruce Line** |
|---|---|---|
| Share of sales | ~75% | ~25% |
| Product type | **Licensed** — carries IP from Disney, Marvel, etc. | **Generic / original** — no licensed IP |
| Former name | — | "Edge" (still appears in old records) |
| Defining constraint | Every design needs **licensor approval** twice (concept, then sample) | No licensor; **buyer approval only** |
| Run by | Jessica (PM) + Liz (Creative Director) | Jen (Creative Director) |
| Process weight | Heavyweight, many gated stages | Lightweight, fewer stages |

**The licensors (POP only):** Disney, Marvel, Star Wars (LucasFilm), Warner Bros, DC Comics, NBCUniversal, Paramount/Nickelodeon, Peanuts, SEGA, Strawberry Shortcake (WildBrain CPLP), WWE, One Piece (TOEI), Care Bears, Coca-Cola, Sesame Street. Each licensor owns "properties" (e.g., within Disney: "Mickey Mouse," "Mickey and Friends," "Princess Tiana") and publishes **style guides** that dictate how their characters and assets may be used — and *when* (style guides are date-gated to selling seasons).

## 3. How work begins

A new product effort starts one of three ways:

1. **A customer request** — a retail buyer asks sales for a specific product type, format, or a refresh of an existing line. Sales relays the brief to the PM.
2. **An internal line refresh** — the company decides on its own to refresh a line (new designs, new licensors or properties, a new product category) and presents it to buyers afterward.
3. **New product development** — the team develops a brand-new product format or category into a full concept before seeking buyer interest.

Where the *ideas* come from (Jessica): "a shopping trip to one of the retailers our client or we want it to be, an offer from a factory, inspiration from internet, a mix from existent product formats we already have."

**Two secondary sales channels exist alongside direct retail:**
- **Wholesale sublicensors** — online sellers (e.g., Stallion Art, Iconick) buy the product and resell it under their own brand, sublicensing the IP. Their designs still go through the **full licensor approval process**. These are designed by outside teams, which makes them unusually hard to manage (see §9).
- **Internal / new development** — products built proactively, then presented.

# PART A — POP CREATIONS: The Licensed Product Journey

## 4. The two things the business is really tracking

Everything in POP is one of two objects.

**An offer (a "project").** One offer, to one buyer, at one retailer, for one season. For example: *"Julie Greer at Burlington for Valentines 2027,"* or *"Alice Zhu at Dollar General for Fall/Winter 2026."* An offer carries the **brief**: the buyer, the retailer, the season, which licensors and properties are in play, which product types the buyer wants, the on-shelf date, and any restrictions. It exists so the creative team has a single reference and doesn't have to memorize the details. As Jessica put it, "each buyer purchases certain types of products and specific properties within each license. All this information is included… as a brief so the creative designer can refer to it during the process."

The brief carries real constraints: **the style guides used to source the design assets must be valid for the date the product will be on the shelf** — licensors date-gate their style guides to selling seasons.

**A picked product (a "SKU").** When a buyer selects a design from a presentation, that selection becomes a specific product to be built, carried all the way through approval and production. A picked product carries: its full approval history, its link back to the offer it came from, its licensor submission numbers, its sample photos, and the order history of which retailers bought it and when.

There is also a third, quieter category the business currently *loses*: **the preliminary designs that were shown to a buyer but not picked.** These are real creative work, but today they vanish (see §10 — this is the company's single biggest pain point).

## 5. The full journey, step by step

Here is the path a licensed product travels, and who touches it at each step.

| # | Step | Who does it | What happens |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Brief / offer created** | Sales + PM | An offer is defined for a buyer/retailer/season with its restrictions, product types, properties, and dates. |
| 2 | **Preliminary designs created** | Creative Designer | The creative team designs concept options for the buyer, working from the brief. |
| 3 | **Presentation to buyer** | Sales (Adam) | Sales shows the preliminary designs to the buyer. |
| 4 | **Buyer picks + format chosen** | Buyer | The buyer selects designs and picks the **format** for each — material, size, specifications. Only now does a pick become a product to build. |
| 5 | **Product (SKU) created + description written** | Creative Designer | Each pick becomes a tracked product. The creative designer writes the description (material, size, artwork description) and prepares the final art files. |
| 6 | **Licensing sheet + packaging built** | Technical Designer | Using the art files, the technical designer builds the **licensing sheet** (the formal spec the licensor reviews) and designs the packaging. |
| 7 | **Internal Creative Director review** | Creative Director (Liz) | **Nothing goes to a licensor without Liz's approval.** She reviews the licensing sheet and the design. This is the internal gate. |
| 8 | **Concept submitted to licensor** | Licensing Team | The licensing team submits the concept and packaging to the licensor's portal. |
| 9 | **Licensor responds** | Licensor | The licensor either **approves**, **rejects with revisions**, or **approves with changes** (approved, but minor revisions are required before sampling). |
| 10a | **Order placed (PO received)** | Buyer → Technical Designer | The buyer places a purchase order; files are prepared for the factory. |
| 10b | **OR sample requested by sales** | Sales → Technical Designer | No order yet, but sales wants a physical sample to close the deal; files are prepared for the factory. *(10a and 10b are parallel paths into sampling.)* |
| 11 | **Sample requested from factory** | Factory | Tech packs are sent to a factory, which produces a pre-production sample (PPS). |
| 12 | **Sample reviewed internally** | Creative Director + Licensing Team | The factory sends PPS photos. Liz and the licensing team review them before anything goes back to the licensor. |
| 13 | **Factory resample (if needed)** | Factory | If errors are found, the sample is corrected and re-photographed. |
| 14 | **Sample submitted to licensor (PPS)** | Licensing Team | A clean sample is submitted to the licensor for PPS approval. |
| 15 | **Sample revision (if licensor requests)** | Creative + Technical Designer | The licensor may request changes to the sample. |
| 16 | **Pre-production approved (PPS approved)** | Licensor | The licensor approves the sample. **Mass production is now authorized.** |
| 17 | **Mass production** | Factory | The factory produces the order. |
| 18 | **Production approved / closed** | Licensing Team | All licensor requirements are satisfied and the submission is closed. *Requirements vary by licensor* — some need a product-safety report, some need the physical sample shipped to their office, some are satisfied with PPS approval alone. |
| 19 | **Shipping & import** | Production Team | The licensor's submission form (the "Brand Assurance" record) is printed and attached to the shipping boxes alongside the trademark authorization, so the goods can ship and clear import. |

A product is **"approved"** at step 9 only when the licensor returns it with no feedback or corrections. A product is **"finished"** at step 18 (Production Approved).

## 6. The licensor relationship in depth

This is the defining feature of the licensed line. Two separate approvals are required: **concept approval** (before any sample is made) and **sample / PPS approval** (before mass production).

**What a licensor checks at concept (Jessica):**
- Correct use of assets — no mixing different style guides, no distorting or modifying the characters.
- Use of color.
- **Storytelling** — the design must tell a story; no plain backgrounds, no unrelated assets thrown together.
- Packaging against the licensor's guidelines.
- The on-shelf date — the style guide used must be valid for the selling window.

**Who talks to the licensor:** only the licensing team (manager + coordinator). Packaging revisions are handled by technical design; creative revisions by the creative designer. **Everything the licensing team submits is first approved internally by Liz.**

**Licensors respond at very different speeds**, which materially affects planning. Expected turnaround per licensor (Jessica):

| Licensor | Expected response |
|---|---|
| Disney | 1–3 days |
| LucasFilm / Star Wars | 1–2 days |
| Marvel | 1–2 days |
| Nickelodeon (Paramount) | 3–6 days |
| Sesame Street | 4 days |
| Coca-Cola | 6 days |
| NBC Universal | 5–7 days |
| Warner Brothers | 5–10 days |
| Peanuts | 7–10 days |
| SEGA | 7–10 days |
| Strawberry Shortcake (WildBrain CPLP) | 7–10 days |
| WWE | 7–10 days |
| Care Bears | 7–10 days |
| One Piece (TOEI) | 7–15 days |

Some licensors are also far more demanding on revisions than others — SEGA in particular tends to require at least one concept revision on essentially everything, while Disney and Star Wars approve most concepts cleanly.

### Compliance artifacts

**Brand Assurance.** When a new item is submitted, the licensor's portal generates a **submission number** — the Brand Assurance number. The licensing team records it (linking it to the product) and saves the submission PDF. It's needed a *second* time at production: the production team prints the Brand Assurance form and pastes it on the shipping boxes alongside the trademark authorization so the goods can be imported. So it is **both a submission record and a shipping-compliance document.** *(Notably, Liz — the Creative Director — has never heard of it; it lives entirely with the licensing team.)*

**PI / Product Integrity.** A test report certifying that the materials used (and their quantities) are non-toxic, non-hazardous, and safe. **Only some licensors require it.** When required, the licensing team receives the report by email and submits it to the licensor. Because most licensors don't require it, it is tracked inconsistently today.

## 7. Costing, sourcing, and manufacturing constraints

Before a product can be quoted or built, a **costing sheet** is created — and this is the source of a lot of downstream rework.

- A **technical designer** creates the costing sheet using internet references, photos from shopping trips, and product offers from factories.
- The **art director (Liz) approves the costing sheet** before it goes to factories for quotes.
- The **sourcing team / China team** reviews it to confirm or clarify restrictions, construction, and materials, and **obtains the die lines**.

The problem: the **manufacturing constraints** — die lines, the number of colors a factory can print, the printing technique, where legal lines must be placed — are often **known only to the art director**, who "usually specifies the restrictions, but sometimes forgets to share them with the creative team, or sometimes she's unaware of them." The mismatch is then discovered during sampling, forcing the design and licensing sheet to be redone. When both the creative and technical designers understand the product-type constraints up front (based on the agreed factory cost), products go through with far fewer revisions.

## 9. Reuse, multi-buyer dynamics, and closure

**Selling the same product to more than one buyer.** Most licensors allow a product to be sold to multiple buyers over time. **Some buyers prohibit resale** of what they've bought — a restriction the business must respect per buyer. There is also a **time constraint** on re-offering. Today reuse happens only case-by-case and only occasionally, when someone remembers the product exists. The product keeps its identity (its code is the licensor's approval record) and should carry the history of which retailers ordered it.

**Two buyers pick the same design at once.** When this happens, the team notices manually. The fix: the creative designer makes a **second version with minor changes** (different icons, image sizes, colors, or embellishments), and sales presents the variation to one of the two buyers — without explaining the real reason.

**Closing or canceling a product.** A product should be canceled, with a reason, when:
- the factory quote doesn't meet the company's cost target (**cost**),
- the product isn't under contract with the licensor (**licensing**),
- a manufacturing problem was found during sampling — construction impossible, too difficult, or too expensive (**sampling**),
- the buyer declined and no other buyer is interested (**buyer**).

A product that reaches Production Approved is **completed** — the happy path. The business problem today: there is **no formal way to cancel**, so dead products sit open indefinitely, some for four to five years.

**Approved-but-unsold concepts.** A large pool of concepts get licensor approval but then stall — the buyer passed on price, the production timing didn't fit, or the order was canceled. These are **not dead**; they are approved inventory that should be re-offered to buyers, but today the work is effectively wasted because nobody re-surfaces it.

## 10. Where the POP process breaks today (process problems, in the team's words)

**1. Lost preliminary designs — the #1 pain point.** Designs shown to a buyer but not picked are tied to that one buyer's presentation and effectively disappear. *"Designs that are lost are lost several times a week. We've tried to reuse these designs for other projects… but it involves manually searching through past presentations"* (Jessica). The team only ever organizes what was *selected*; the rest is lost creative work.

**2. The Creative Director bottleneck.** Liz's review-and-approval step is the structural chokepoint, for five distinct reasons (Jessica):
1. **Volume** — licensing sheets pile up when Liz is busy with her other responsibilities.
2. **Late design changes** — Liz sometimes changes artwork she already approved at the presentation stage, which forces the entire licensing sheet to be rebuilt.
3. **Product-type unfamiliarity** — she sometimes has to consult senior creative, sales, production, or sourcing in China before she can approve a product type, which adds days.
4. **Color preferences** — colors the creative designer explicitly called out get sent back for her preference.
5. **Property / packaging mismatch** — the wrong property gets used (e.g., "Mickey and Friends" art on a "Mickey Mouse" submission), and the work is returned until corrected.

**3. Capacity and surges.** Jessica's core daily job is allocating limited designers across competing deadlines. Surges break this: a flood of Hobby Lobby picks once outran the creative team's ability to deliver art (worsened by unfamiliar new product types), forcing overtime, pulling creatives off other projects, and sending samples *without* approvals to keep up; a huge Ollies order forced pausing everything else.

**4. Knowledge trapped in people's heads.** *"Information is very much in people's heads. Communication isn't this company's strong suit, and records of certain things are scattered everywhere… Many people don't understand the complete workflow… sometimes there are rework processes because the wrong person does the task"* (Jessica).

**5. Unclear ownership of scheduling.** The art director sometimes assigns tasks and projects, which is the PM's role, *"which throws off my deadlines"* (Jessica). (This is a genuine tension, not just a mistake — Liz legitimately knows which designer is strong at which product, so she does help direct work.)

**6. Manufacturing constraints surface too late.** As in §7 — constraints known only to the art director aren't shared with designers until sampling reveals the mismatch.

## 11. How Jessica and Liz wish the POP process ran

- **Nothing creative is ever lost.** Every design — picked or not — stays findable and reusable, searchable by license, property, product type, and season, so the team can re-offer past work instead of starting from scratch.
- **The business gets ahead of the season instead of scrambling.** *"Project management should be proactive, not reactive… Instead of waiting for a buyer to request products for next year's Valentine's Day or Christmas and then scrambling, we should have offered the products beforehand"* (Jessica). The company already knows its seasons — Valentine's, Easter, Graduation, Back to School, Harvest, Halloween, Christmas, plus Everyday — and its history of which buyers order what. Approved-but-unsold concepts should be proactively packaged into seasonal offers.
- **Designers see their constraints while they design.** Die lines, color counts, printing techniques, and materials should be visible at design time, so the art director is no longer a single point of relay and rework drops.
- **Each person owns and advances their own step**, with clear handoffs to the next person — so the PM manages exceptions instead of pushing every product through every stage by hand. For this to work, updating has to be effortless and incremental (you report the first 5 of 20 art files as they're done, not all 20 at the end).
- **Liz keeps her judgment but loses the chaos.** She doesn't want a rigid checklist imposed on her aesthetic review — that runs on 20 years of expertise. She *does* want her submissions tracked in one place instead of scattered across messages, and she wants product specs (how each product is made) in front of her when she reviews.

# PART B — SPRUCE LINE: The Generic Product Journey

*Spruce Line (formerly "Edge") is the company's non-licensed, original-design business. There is no licensor anywhere in this process — the only external approval is the buyer's.*

## 12. The three things the business is tracking

Spruce is organized differently from POP. Jen tracks three distinct kinds of thing:

**A design collection (a trend/art theme).** A body of original art on a theme — "Gaming," "Farmhouse," "Cowgirl Country," "Soft Religion" — that is **available to all accounts**, not tied to any one buyer. Collections are organized by format and then by theme (e.g., under "Wall Art": Gaming, Farmhouse, Kitchen Art, Sneaker Art, Automotive, Florals, Coastal, Abstracts, and many more; under "Floor Coverings": Coir Mats, Spring Coir Mats, Holiday Coir Mats). A single trend collection can hold **hundreds of designs**, and the **high-resolution art isn't even produced until a buyer asks for it.** Collections are the raw creative inventory; they are not yet products.

**A project (account-specific work).** One project for one buyer/account — for example, "Forman Mills, buyer Jennifer, Wall Art" or "Burlington, buyer Anna, New Formats." A project is where a specific buyer's selections and brief live.

**A style-numbered product.** When a buyer actually commits — selects specific designs and either places an order or requests a sample — those selections become **style-numbered products**. The style number is the product's permanent identity, and all of that product's files are findable by it. **Only style-numbered items are "real products."** The hundreds of designs in a trend collection are not.

## 13. The journey, step by step

| # | Step | Who | What happens |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Theme/collection development** | Jen + design team | The team develops trend and art themes. They meet twice a month — once for art, once for product — to decide what to develop. Jen sends a recap of who works on what. |
| 2 | **AI art screening** | Nat (Junior Designer) | For AI-generated art, Nat removes poor-quality pieces and obvious AI mistakes before Jen reviews. |
| 3 | **Presentation finalized** | Jen | Jen finalizes the presentation before it goes to sales. |
| 4 | **Presented to buyer** | Adam (Sales) | Adam presents to the buyer and gathers any requested art changes. |
| 5 | **Selections captured** | Jen + team | After the meeting the team builds a **selections document** of everything the buyer chose. |
| 6 | **Account-specific next step** | varies by account | What happens next depends entirely on the account (see §14). |
| 7 | **Style number assigned (commitment)** | Jen + team | Once the buyer commits to a size/format and an order (or a sample for sample-accounts), each selection gets a **style number** — it becomes a real product. |
| 8 | **Costing** | Design → Albert/China → Adam | Design makes a costing tech pack; Albert and the China team handle factory selection and costing; **Adam reviews and approves the costing**. |
| 9 | **Sample (if the account requires one)** | Mal | Mal approves sample requests. Some accounts get samples, some don't. |
| 10 | **Order → production** | Factory (via Yuchen) | When there's an order, the team waits for **Yuchen's email** with the factory deadline, then preps and sends files for production. |

**Approvals are entirely internal + buyer.** Jen finalizes; Adam gets buyer sign-off on art changes; once the buyer approves and an order is placed, it goes to production. **Jen and Mal review artwork and tech packs**; Jen handles purchase-order review for accuracy; Mal handles sample-request approvals. There is no licensor anywhere.

## 14. Accounts behave differently — and the process must flex

The unit of work after a buyer meeting depends on the account. Two real examples (Jen):

- **Burlington (buyer Anna) — no samples.** Meeting → selections document → Anna uses it to make her final selections for an order → **only then** are style numbers assigned. This account never gets samples.
- **Hobby Lobby (buyer Kyle) — samples required.** Meeting → selections document → the team internally picks a size and format → Kyle approves or requests changes → style numbers are assigned → **samples are requested.**

Most trend collections are **not** account-specific — **except all storage designs and all Hobby Lobby work**, which are. This account-by-account variation is exactly why Jen organizes her work by **lifecycle stage** rather than by buyer: at a glance she needs to see where each piece of work is and what the next step is.

## 15. Seasons, cancellation, reuse, and cross-line flow

**Seasons are account-specific.** Spruce does *not* follow POP's fixed retail calendar. Hobby Lobby works to a dated calendar; Burlington requests verbally at meetings. The team updates its seasonal presentations **starting with Hobby Lobby** and adapts from there.

**Cancellation.** The most common reason a Spruce design never reaches production is simply that **the buyer doesn't move forward** — projects sit "with the buyer for approval" for weeks or months. Adam and Jen review the full list **once a quarter** and remove the stalled ones. As with POP, there's no formal cancel step.

**Reuse.** Design collections are reused across all accounts by design — that's their entire purpose. Once something is a style-numbered product it belongs to a specific buyer, and multi-buyer reuse isn't tracked the way it is in POP.

**Reactive vs. proactive.** Usually the buyer reviews themes the company has already developed and selects from them; occasionally a buyer asks for a specific theme the company doesn't have yet (e.g., "Bears with Sweaters").

**Cross-line.** The two businesses **share product development but not art** — a product format might be shared, but licensed and generic artwork stay separate.

## 16. Where the Spruce process breaks today

- **Chasing people.** Jen's single biggest frustration: *"Having to chase down information or remind people to complete their part of the product lifecycle."*
- **Factory opacity.** Factories aren't transparent about their capabilities, and sometimes **change pricing during the pricing stage in ways that aren't revealed until samples have already been requested** — wasting work.
- **No timeline tracking.** The design team doesn't track order timelines at all; the only hard date is the factory deadline that arrives in Yuchen's email. Buyers (especially Burlington) often place orders at the last minute, leaving little prep time.
- **No revision tracking.** Buyer change requests travel by message, marked-up file, or email, with nothing formally recorded.
- **A failed freelancer experiment.** An attempt to develop new art through many freelancers became unmanageable; most work was brought back in-house and the freelancer pool was culled to a few reliable people.

## 17. How Jen wishes the Spruce process ran

- **Everyone on one consistent system, and sticking to it.** *"Having all team members using the same system for tracking and sticking with it would make everything more organized."* The discipline matters as much as the tool.
- **Sales can self-serve project status.** A way to give Adam *"the exact status of all projects… so he can easily get answers to his questions"* without Jen assembling it by hand.
- **Get ahead of demand with a design calendar built from history.** Use the timing of past years' projects to anticipate when each account will need what, instead of reacting.
- **Honest, shared visibility into timing** — how long pricing and samples actually take — so the burden of chasing doesn't fall on the design team alone.

Her model of a frictionless account is **Ollies**: the buyer buys everything he selects, works in a single format (canvas), pricing already exists, and he communicates well. The more the process can make every account behave like that, the better.

# PART C — Where the Two Lines Meet

## 18. Shared structure, shared ailments

Although POP and Spruce run different processes, they share people (Adam in sales; Albert and the China team for costing and factories; Yuchen for factory deadlines), share product development (but never artwork), and suffer several of the **same underlying process ailments**:

| Ailment | POP (Jessica/Liz) | Spruce (Jen) |
|---|---|---|
| **Creative work / status gets lost or scattered** | preliminary designs lost; records spread across messages, email, and shared servers | chasing people for status; nothing in one place |
| **Stalled work piles up with no formal closure** | approved-but-unsold concepts; products open 4–5 years | projects sit "with buyer" for months; quarterly manual cull |
| **No record of revisions** | feedback lives in messages and markups | change requests live in messages and emails |
| **Reacting instead of planning the season** | scrambling each season | wants a design calendar from history |
| **Constraints/pricing surface too late** | manufacturing constraints known only to the art director | factory pricing changes hidden until sampling |
| **Sales needs a clean status picture** | "list of all projects for the same retailer" | "a list for Adam on the exact status of all projects" |

The deepest shared wish across all three people is the same: **one place where the work lives, nothing gets lost, and the team can get ahead of the season instead of chasing it.**

## Appendix — Glossary

- **Licensor** — the IP owner (Disney, Marvel, etc.) that must approve licensed designs and samples. POP only.
- **Property** — a specific franchise/character set within a licensor (e.g., "Mickey Mouse" vs. "Mickey and Friends").
- **Style guide** — the licensor's rulebook for using its assets, gated to selling seasons by date.
- **Project / offer** — one offer to one buyer at one retailer for one season (POP); one account-specific body of work (Spruce).
- **SKU** (POP) — a specific picked product carried through approval and production.
- **Style number** (Spruce) — the permanent identity of a committed Spruce product.
- **Design collection** (Spruce) — an account-agnostic trend/art theme containing many designs.
- **Licensing sheet** — the formal product spec the licensor reviews. POP only.
- **Brand Assurance** — the licensor's submission number; also the compliance form attached to shipping boxes. POP only.
- **PI / Product Integrity** — a materials-safety test report some licensors require. POP only.
- **PPS** — pre-production sample; the physical sample the licensor approves before mass production. POP only.
- **On-shelf date** — when a product must be in stores. **PPS-requested date** — when a buyer wants to see the physical sample (often weeks earlier). Two different deadlines.
- **Costing sheet** — the document that establishes what a product will cost to make, built before factory quotes.
